# World Scale Systems Atlas

## Summary

This atlas maps global tonal logic for DreamChamber.

It covers:

- scale families
- modal systems
- tuning logics
- drone and recitation structures
- harmony systems
- microtonal practices

It should not be read as one universal theory of pitch.

Different traditions organize pitch through different priorities:

- scale degrees
- melodic pathways
- reciting tones
- drone relationships
- interval color
- ornament behavior
- harmonic function

## Reading Rule

DreamChamber should not reduce every tradition to Western equal-tempered note names.

The system has to preserve at least five different ways of thinking about pitch:

1. fixed scale degrees
2. modal centers and reciting tones
3. pathway-based melodic movement
4. drone-defined interval gravity
5. harmony-defined chord function

## Macro Tonal Families

### Pentatonic

- anhemitonic pentatonic fields
- hemitonic pentatonic variants
- layered pentatonic systems across Africa, East Asia, Andes, and Celtic traditions

### Heptatonic

- diatonic and church-mode families
- raga- and maqam-adjacent seven-note fields
- equidistant or near-equidistant heptatonic organizations in parts of Southeast Asia

### Modal And Recitational

- maqam
- makam
- dastgah
- qenet
- raga
- liturgical and chant recitation systems

### Drone-Centered

- South Asian drone gravity
- overtone-based fields
- pipe-and-harp drone traditions
- polyphonic drone singing

### Harmony-Centered

- European tonal harmony
- gospel harmony
- jazz harmonic extension
- montuno and vamp-based harmonic fields

### Microtonal And Intonation-Sensitive

- shruti nuance
- maqam and makam comma-like interval inflection
- dastgah color
- non-equal metallophone and gong-based tunings
- adaptive just intonation and overtone alignment

## Regional Tonal Systems

### West Africa And Sahel

Common pitch logics:

- pentatonic and heptatonic fields
- ostinato-centered melodic cells
- praise-song contour systems
- call-and-response pitch framing

Important rule:

Pitch often works together with rhythm and speech contour rather than as isolated scalar display.

### Horn Of Africa

Common pitch logics:

- Ethiopian qenet families such as tezeta, bati, ambassel, and anchihoye
- bittersweet modal gravity
- lyric-led contour and cadence

Important rule:

Mode identity is often inseparable from memory, text, and emotional color.

### North Africa And Arab Worlds

Common pitch logics:

- maqam families such as Rast, Bayati, Hijaz, Nahawand, and Saba
- jins-based segment thinking
- recitation and ornament as structural, not decorative

Important rule:

DreamChamber should model pathway and interval color, not only a static scale row.

### Anatolia

Common pitch logics:

- makam systems
- seyir or characteristic melodic path
- pitch nuance by direction and phrase role

Important rule:

The same pitch collection can behave differently depending on ascent, descent, emphasis, and cadence.

### Persia

Common pitch logics:

- dastgah and avaz systems
- gusheh pathways within radif organization
- nuanced microtonal inflection

Important rule:

Mode here is closer to a landscape of melodic pathways than a single scale chart.

### Caucasus

Common pitch logics:

- drone and close-harmony stacks
- lament inflection
- mugham-related scalar gravity in Azerbaijani contexts

Important rule:

Polyphony, tension, and drone relation matter as much as abstract note inventory.

### Central Asia And Steppe

Common pitch logics:

- pentatonic expanses
- open-fifth and overtone-friendly fields
- long-song interval spacing

Important rule:

Large spatial feeling and overtone resonance are part of the tonal logic.

### Siberia And Arctic

Common pitch logics:

- overtone centers
- narrow-range motif cells
- call-and-echo contour

Important rule:

The voice itself may define the pitch system more than an external instrument scale.

### South Asia

Common pitch logics:

- raga systems
- thaat and mela classification frameworks
- shruti nuance
- strong ascent and descent identity
- drone-defined tonal center

Important rule:

Raga is not just a scale. It includes:

- characteristic tones
- melodic pathways
- phrase gravity
- ornament behavior
- time, mood, or devotional association

### Himalaya And Tibetan Sphere

Common pitch logics:

- pentatonic and drone-centered fields
- overtone concentration
- chant-based interval focus

Important rule:

Resonance and ritual pacing often matter more than scalar density.

### China

Common pitch logics:

- pentatonic frameworks often described through gong, shang, jue, zhi, and yu centers
- additional changing tones in broader heptatonic practice
- ornamental bends and sliding pitch calligraphy

Important rule:

Pitch class identity should remain linked to contour, timbre, and phrase shape.

### Japan

Common pitch logics:

- ryo and ritsu court-derived modal ideas
- in and yo pentatonic color
- ma as spatial silence in pitch design

Important rule:

Absence, attack, and bend are part of the tonal system.

### Korea

Common pitch logics:

- pyeongjo
- gyemyeonjo
- sustained bending centers
- narrative and declamatory melodic pull

Important rule:

The emotional weight of bent tones is part of mode identity.

### Southeast Asia

Common pitch logics:

- slendro
- pelog
- pathet
- Thai and Lao heptatonic organizations
- kulintang modal centers
- heterophonic elaboration over core melodic frameworks

Important rule:

Non-equal tuning and ensemble-specific pitch worlds must not be forced into one normalized scale.

### Mediterranean, Iberia, And Romani Routes

Common pitch logics:

- Phrygian and flamenco color
- lament tetrachords
- Ottoman-route modal overlap
- melismatic descent and cadence

Important rule:

Gesture, vocal attack, and cadence formulas matter as much as pitch inventory.

### Balkans And Black Sea

Common pitch logics:

- asymmetric dance phrase grouping
- modal dance cells
- makam-influenced local variants

Important rule:

The meter and the mode often feel fused.

### Western, Central, And Northern Europe

Common pitch logics:

- church modes
- major and minor tonal systems
- drone-based folk modes
- close-harmony choral structures

Important rule:

DreamChamber should separate modal folk logic from later harmony-heavy art-music logic.

### Indigenous North America

Common pitch logics:

- nation-specific song systems
- melody tied to breath and chant
- recurring interval sets that vary by nation, ceremony, and social role

Important rule:

There is no single pan-Indigenous scale theory.
Context and nation matter.

### Mesoamerica

Common pitch logics:

- chant cells
- invocation contour
- son-based modal loops in later folk continuities

Important rule:

Ceremonial and vernacular layers should stay distinct.

### Andes

Common pitch logics:

- anhemitonic pentatonic fields
- parallel panpipe motion
- lament turns over pentatonic cores

Important rule:

Altitude, breath, and ensemble hocket are part of the tonal feel.

### Amazonia

Common pitch logics:

- chant cells
- invocation pivots
- drone references
- contour shaped by breath and environmental call

Important rule:

Directionality and voice-leading function can matter more than a fixed abstract scale.

### Caribbean And Creole Systems

Common pitch logics:

- vamp and montuno logic
- horn-riff cells
- chant-hook release points
- dub space altering harmonic perception

Important rule:

Harmony, groove, and arrangement density are inseparable from pitch identity.

### Brazil

Common pitch logics:

- chord-melodic interplay
- chant and response hooks
- rich syncopated harmonic motion in choro, bossa, and related forms

Important rule:

Rhythmic placement changes how harmony is heard.

### North American Roots, Gospel, Jazz, And Blues

Common pitch logics:

- blue-note inflection
- pentatonic hymn cores
- modal fiddle loops
- gospel harmonic lift
- jazz extension and chromatic reharmonization

Important rule:

Bend, swing, and harmonic substitution are structural.

## Unknown And Future Scale Frontiers

This section is speculative and should stay marked as such.

### Lost And Unarchived

- village-specific tunings that never entered formal notation
- private ceremonial pitch systems
- acoustic-space-dependent interval worlds

### Future And DreamChamber-Native

- adaptive just-intonation systems driven by participant motion
- vulnerability-aware scales tied to Nerve-to-Note intent behavior
- machine-native overtone maps
- planetary or synthetic resonance sets for speculative biomes

## DreamChamber Implications

- scales should be selectable by region and by system type
- the system must support pathway-based modes, not just note collections
- drone, ornament, and cadence behavior should be runtime parameters
- "unknown world" scales must remain clearly speculative
