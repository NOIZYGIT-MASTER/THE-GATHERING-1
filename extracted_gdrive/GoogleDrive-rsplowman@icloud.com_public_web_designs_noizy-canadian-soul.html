The Hall of Canadian Creators

AIVA takes every new visitor through this hall before showing them anything else. You cannot understand why NOIZY exists without first understanding what Canada made.

* * *
David Cronenberg
Toronto, Ontario · Film · Body Horror · Philosophy
Videodrome. The Fly. Naked Lunch. A History of Violence. Crimes of the Future. He did not make horror films. He made philosophical investigations into the relationship between the human body and technology — dressed in horror because that was the most honest costume. He is the most distinctly Canadian filmmaker who ever worked, because he asked the most Canadian question: what happens to being human when the machine becomes part of the body?
"I think the job of the artist is to ask questions, not to answer them."
Norman Jewison
Toronto, Ontario · Film · Justice · Humanity
In the Heat of the Night (Academy Award, Best Picture, 1967). Fiddler on the Roof. Moonstruck. He made films about justice, dignity, and the weight of being an outsider. He was offered The Godfather. He turned it down, saying Francis Ford Coppola should make it. That is a very Canadian decision. He founded the Canadian Film Centre, which trained the next generation of Canadian filmmakers. He was the most generous filmmaker Canada produced.
"Cinema is a mirror by which we often see ourselves."
James Cameron
Kapuskasing, Ontario · Film · Epic · Ocean
The Terminator. Aliens. Titanic (highest-grossing film in history, 1997). Avatar (highest-grossing film in history, 2009 and again 2022). He drove a truck to California. He made the three highest-grossing films in cinema history from a childhood in small-town Ontario. He also dove to the Titanic wreck 33 times and descended alone to the deepest point in the ocean in a submarine he helped design. He is quietly Canadian about all of it.
"Imagination is a force that can actually manifest a reality."
Denis Villeneuve
Gentilly, Québec · Film · Sci-Fi · Emotion
Incendies. Prisoners. Arrival. Blade Runner 2049. Dune: Part One and Part Two. The most technically ambitious Canadian filmmaker of his generation and the most emotionally intelligent. His films ask: what does a human being owe to another human being? What does an empire owe the people it consumes? These are Canadian questions.
Glenn Gould
Toronto, Ontario · Classical Piano · Philosopher
Recorded the Goldberg Variations in 1955 at age 22 and redefined how Bach could sound. Recorded them again in 1981, the year he died, and redefined them again. Stopped performing live in 1964 because he believed the recording studio was a superior instrument. Wore fingerless gloves year-round. Hummed audibly during recordings. Believed the North had shaped his music. He was right.
"The purpose of art is the gradual, lifelong construction of a state of wonder and serenity."
Oscar Peterson
Montréal, Québec · Little Burgundy · Jazz Piano
The greatest jazz pianist who ever played. This is not a debatable statement among jazz pianists who have heard him play. Grew up in Little Burgundy, Montréal. 200 albums. Dizzy Gillespie called him the Maharaja of the keyboard. His fingers moved faster than perception. He made Montréal sound like everywhere music had ever lived simultaneously.
Joni Mitchell
Fort Macleod, Alberta · Singer-Songwriter · Painter
The greatest songwriter of the 20th century. Prince said she was his favourite musician. Blue is the most intimate album ever recorded. "A Case of You" is the most perfect song ever written. She invented her own guitar tunings. She painted every album cover. She contracted polio as a child and survived. All of this is in the music. This is a Canadian opinion, held carefully and absolutely.
"Rock and roll has no memory. The blues has all the memory."
Keith Hampshire
Ottawa, Ontario · Pop · Voice
Ottawa's own. "The One Who Really Loves You." "If You Leave Me Now" — covered internationally. One of the most distinctive pop voices Canada produced in the 1970s. The kind of career that defined the Ottawa sound and the warmth of Canadian pop that never quite got its international due — not because it wasn't good enough, but because Canada never shouted loud enough about its own. His voice sounds like home. Specifically, Ottawa home.
Leonard Cohen
Montréal, Québec · Poetry · Philosophy · Song
"Hallelujah" — covered over 300 times. "Suzanne." "Bird on the Wire." Novelist, poet, songwriter, Buddhist monk. He released his final album at 82, three weeks before he died. The most graceful exit in Canadian music. He is the only musician who makes you feel that poetry and music were always the same thing.
Gordon Lightfoot
Orillia, Ontario · Folk · The Canadian Landscape
"If You Could Read My Mind." "The Wreck of the Edmund Fitzgerald." "Canadian Railroad Trilogy." He wrote the sound of Canada's land into music — the lakes, the forests, the trains, the distances. Bob Dylan said Gordon Lightfoot was his favourite songwriter.
John Candy
Newmarket, Ontario · Comedy · Warmth · SCTV
SCTV. Planes, Trains and Automobiles. Cool Runnings. Uncle Buck. The most beloved Canadian comedian. Not the funniest — the most beloved. Every person who knew him said the same thing: the warmth was real. The generosity was real. He died at 43 in Durango, Mexico, still working. He was 43.
"I'd like to be remembered as a man who gave something back."
The Surrogate Canadians

A Surrogate Canadian is a person who was not born in Canada but who embodies the Canadian sensibility so completely that Canada has retroactively adopted them. They practice peace, love, and understanding at such a sustained level that the nation has decided they count. AIVA greets them by name.

* * *
Fred Rogers
Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania — but his entire philosophy of children's television was shaped directly by Bob Homme, The Friendly Giant, actual Canadian. Rogers cited Homme explicitly. "You are special exactly as you are" is a Canadian philosophy delivered by an American with complete sincerity. Honorary Canadian. Unanimous.
Elvis Costello
"What's so funny 'bout peace, love and understanding?" is the question Canada has been quietly asking since 1867. He asked it loudest and has never stopped. He is a Surrogate Canadian for asking it in every room, without apology, for fifty years.
Nelson Mandela
Made an honorary Canadian citizen in 2001. One of only six people in history to hold this distinction. Reconciliation over revenge. Dignity over dominance. The long arc of justice. Canada recognized this officially and remains proud.
Malala Yousafzai
Honorary Canadian citizen, 2014. Youngest Nobel Peace Prize laureate. Education as a non-negotiable human right. Canada has always been more comfortable making people citizens than making enemies.
Ruth Bader Ginsburg
Said publicly in 2012 that if she were drafting a constitution, she would look at the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms rather than the American Bill of Rights. Canada was moved and has never forgotten this.
Ernie Coombs — Mr. Dressup
Born Lewiston, Maine. Became the most Canadian person who ever lived, and said so himself. Officer of the Order of Canada. 4,000 episodes. The Tickle Trunk. Casey and Finnegan. American-born, completely and utterly Canadian. The arc is perfect.
"You do not need to be from Canada to think like Canada. You just have to act like you actually mean it."

The Canadian Soul — A Manifesto

The world's most important discoveries were made in Canada and given away for a dollar. Insulin — Frederick Banting sold the patent to the University of Toronto for $1 because he refused to profit from a life-saving discovery. The pacemaker — born in Winnipeg from hypothermia research. The electric wheelchair — George Klein at the National Research Council, who also designed aircraft skis, a nuclear reactor, a microsurgical staple gun, and then came out of retirement to consult on the Canadarm. Standard time — Sir Sandford Fleming's idea, now on every clock on earth. Basketball — James Naismith, Almonte, Ontario. The telephone — conceived in Brantford, Ontario, on a vacation. AM radio — Reginald Fessenden, who got the idea watching ripples on a lake near Peterborough and spent nine years perfecting it, then watched his patents get appropriated without consent during a war he was supposed to support.

And then there is the Arrow. The CF-105 Avro Arrow — the most advanced fighter jet in the world in 1958. Mach 2 before any American aircraft could match it. 14,672 people working on it. Cancelled February 20, 1959. All planes cut to pieces. All blueprints destroyed. The engineers went to NASA and helped put Americans on the Moon.

"Canada's relationship with its own greatness follows a consistent pattern: invent something world-changing, then either give it away, have it appropriated, cancel it under external pressure, or simply never mention it again out of characteristic modesty. NOIZY is the infrastructure that refuses to let this pattern continue."
* * *

Canada doesn't shout "look what we did." It just changes things. This is both Canada's most beautiful quality and its most dangerous one. The Friendly Giant never raised his voice. Mr. Dressup never rushed. Glenn Gould hummed quietly while redefining how Bach could sound. Oscar Peterson played the fastest hands in jazz without announcing it. Cirque du Soleil changed what a circus could be without once saying "this is the best circus in the world." It just was. The world noticed eventually.

But "eventually" is the problem. The Arrow was cancelled before eventually arrived. Fessenden never got his. The paint roller inventor died without recognition. Norman Breakey changed how every building on earth gets painted and received nothing and is remembered by almost no one outside Canada.

NOIZY puts the credit in the code. It does not wait for credit to be given.

Bob Homme never condescended to a child. Ernie Coombs never rushed one. The CBC believed rural Canadians deserved to see themselves on television. The Canadian Film Centre that Norman Jewison founded trains filmmakers who could not otherwise afford the training. The Canadian Healthcare system believes that nobody should suffer the consequences of being human because they can't afford a doctor. The Charter of Rights and Freedoms says these rights are not negotiable and they are not for sale.
