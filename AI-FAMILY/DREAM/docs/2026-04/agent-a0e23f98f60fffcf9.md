## TOPIC 1: AI VOICE CLONING LAWSUITS AND LEGAL CASES

### RIAA / Major Labels vs. Suno and Udio

**The Original Filings (June 24, 2024)**
All three major labels — UMG Recordings, Sony Music Entertainment, Warner Music Group — plus the RIAA filed landmark lawsuits simultaneously:
- **UMG et al. v. Suno** — U.S. District Court, District of Massachusetts, Judge F. Dennis Saylor IV (Chief Judge). First case targeting sound recording infringement via AI music generation.
- **UMG et al. v. Udio (Uncharted Labs)** — U.S. District Court, Southern District of New York (SDNY), Judge Alvin K. Hellerstein.

Allegations: mass copyright infringement by training AI models on copyrighted recordings without authorization, and extraction of lyrics from databases (Genius, AZLyrics, Musixmatch) without permission. The central claim is that both platforms trained on vast numbers of sound recordings and reproduced them in outputs.

**Suno's Defense:** Suno filed its answer August 1, 2024, arguing fair use. Does not disclose the dataset used to train its AI.

**Settlement Status as of December 2025:**
- **WMG + Suno**: Settled November 2025. Rolling Stone reported this as a landmark deal. WMG dropped the lawsuit; Suno dropped its fair use defense. Structural terms include: opt-in mechanism for WMG artists (artists choose whether their voices, compositions, likenesses can be used), monthly download caps for paid users. Settlement framed as a "next-generation licensed AI music" partnership.
- **UMG + Udio**: Settled October 2025. New licensed platform planned for 2026. Current Udio service continues with guardrails.
- **WMG + Udio**: Settled November 19, 2025 (TechCrunch). WMG signed deal for AI music platform set to launch 2026.
- **Sony Music + Suno**: ONGOING as of December 2025. Sony has not settled.
- **Sony Music + Udio**: ONGOING as of December 2025. Sony is the sole major label still litigating against Udio per Digital Music News, December 3 and December 18, 2025.

**Additional Suno suits:**
- **GEMA (Germany) v. Suno**: Filed January 2025, ongoing. Munich Regional Court had already ruled November 2025 that OpenAI violated copyright law in training and outputs — favorable signal for GEMA.
- **Koda (Denmark) v. Suno**: Filed November 4, 2025, Copenhagen City Court. First Danish rights organization to sue an AI music service. Claims "biggest theft in music history." Evidence of similarity to works by Aqua, MØ, Christopher. HBS Economics report commissioned by Koda/IFPI Denmark estimates AI-generated music could reduce Danish music industry revenue by 6.9 billion DKK (~$680M USD) from 2025–2030 without policy intervention.
- **Loevy + Loevy Class Action**: Music AI class action on behalf of independent musicians against both companies, ongoing.
- **Justice v. Suno (First Amended Complaint September 22, 2025)**: Individual musician case, filed in Massachusetts, still in early stages.

### Vacker v. ElevenLabs (Voice Actor Case)

**Case:** Vacker v. ElevenLabs, Inc., 1:24-cv-00987, U.S. District Court.

**Plaintiffs:** Voice actors Karissa Vacker and Mark Boyett (audiobook narrators), plus two authors, Brian Larson and Vaughn Heppner (fantasy/sci-fi writers).

**Allegations:** ElevenLabs' voices "Adam" and "Bella" are based on Boyett and Vacker's voices. ElevenLabs trained its AI using recordings from their audiobook narrations, circumventing DRM and encryption protections in violation of the DMCA's anti-circumvention statutes.

**Settlement:** The first settlement in AI copyright litigation. August 23, 2025 — parties told the judge they had reached settlement after mediation. Final long-form agreement expected by September 17, 2025. Case stayed through September 18, 2025, dismissal to follow. Terms not publicly disclosed.

### Bartz v. Anthropic (Authors vs. AI — Largest Copyright Settlement in U.S. History)

**Filed:** Class action, Authors Guild and individual authors.

**Allegation:** Anthropic downloaded millions of pirated books from LibGen and PiLiMi (an Anna's Archive mirror) to train Claude.

**Key Ruling (June 2025):** Judge William Alsup (NDCA) ruled that training on legally acquired copyrighted works = fair use, but that creating a pirated library for general AI training purposes is not fair use. Denied Anthropic's summary judgment on piracy claims.

**Settlement (September 5, 2025):** $1.5 billion — the largest U.S. copyright settlement in history. Preliminarily approved by Judge Alsup September 25, 2025. Covers approximately 500,000 copyrighted works (~$3,000 per work). Settlement website: www.anthropiccopyrightsettlement.com.

### Andersen v. Stability AI (Visual Artists)

**Filed:** January 13, 2023, NDCA, Judge William H. Orrick. Plaintiffs: Sarah Andersen and other visual artists. August 2024: Federal judge refused to dismiss core copyright claims, allowing case into discovery. As of November 2025: parties exchanging documents, plaintiffs seeking additional custodians/search terms. Surviving claims: direct copyright infringement, trademark, trade dress, inducement.

### Getty Images v. Stability AI (UK)

**Judgment:** November 4, 2025, High Court of England and Wales.

**Outcome:** Getty LOST its secondary copyright infringement claim. Getty voluntarily withdrew primary copyright infringement claims (related to the training itself) before trial concluded — meaning the court never ruled on whether training on copyrighted images violates copyright. The court found "extremely limited" trademark infringement (early Stable Diffusion versions reproduced Getty watermarks) but dismissed the main trademark claim under Section 10(3). Critical finding: Getty's copyright-protected works were factually used to train Stable Diffusion, wherever training took place — but this was not adjudicated as infringement.

**Significance:** First UK court ruling on AI training and copyright. Mixed result — not the creator-protective precedent advocates hoped for.

### Lehrman v. Lovo (Voice Actors)

U.S. case. Two professional voice actors sued Lovo (AI TTS company) claiming Lovo solicited recordings under false pretenses (promising non-commercial internal research use). Court declined to dismiss right of publicity claims under New York Civil Rights Law. The recently added "digital replica" provision to New York's Civil Rights Law could plausibly apply to AI-generated voice clones.

### Arijit Singh v. Codible Ventures (India — Landmark)

2024. Bollywood singer Arijit Singh sued AI company in India. Bombay High Court ruled that Singh's name, voice, photograph, likeness, vocal style, vocal technique, vocal arrangements, mannerisms, manner of singing, and signature are all protected facets of his personality rights. First Indian judgment addressing misuse of generative AI in music. Significant international precedent.
