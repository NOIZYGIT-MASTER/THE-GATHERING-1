# ONE MAN'S LOVE
### A 15-Month Memoir of Disappearing Into the Machine and Building My Way Back Out

*"What's so funny 'bout peace, love and understanding?"*
— Nick Lowe. Performed by Elvis Costello. Covered by Dave Edmunds. Lived by Rob Plowman.

---

## THE PREMISE

A musician disappears.

Not from the stage. Not from the world. He disappears into the technological quicksand — that slow, pulling, disorienting feeling of watching the industry you built your life in get swallowed whole by something none of us had a name for yet. The ground shifts. The tools change. The rules vanish. And one morning you wake up and you don't know what you are anymore.

This is the story of what happened next.

Not a tech book. Not a self-help book. Not a business book — though it is all of those things in parts. It is a human story. One man rebuilding his entire life and his entire body of work, piece by piece, from the inside of a technological revolution, and then turning around and handing what he built back to the people he built it for.

Because there is absolutely nothing funny about peace, love and understanding. There never was. We just need a little bit more of it.

---

## THE SHAPE OF THE BOOK

### PROLOGUE — The Quicksand

The moment a musician realizes he is being swallowed. Not by failure. Not by irrelevance. By time, and by the sheer velocity of what was coming. What it feels like from the inside when the world you understood shifts beneath your feet and doesn't shift back.

The quicksand is not dramatic. That is the terrifying part. It is quiet. It is gradual. It is waking up one morning and realizing the instrument is still in your hands but the room around it has completely changed.

---

### PART ONE — THE DISAPPEARING
*Chapters 1–4*

**Chapter 1 — Who I Was**
The life before. The music. The career. The creative identity built over decades. Not a biography — a portrait. The tools a musician uses to navigate the world. The community. The craft. What it means to make something and put it into people's ears and hearts.

**Chapter 2 — The Tsunami Arrives**
AI does not knock. It does not announce itself. It arrives the way a wave does — first a strange pulling back of the water, a weird quiet, and then everything at once. The music industry, the voice industry, the creative economy — all of it hit simultaneously.

**Chapter 3 — The Paralysis**
What happens when you are a creator and you suddenly cannot tell what creation is worth anymore. The silence. The inertia. The feeling of being technically present but functionally gone. The quicksand doing its work.

**Chapter 4 — The Choice**
The moment that changes everything is never as cinematic as you expect. It is usually small. A question. A conversation. A stupid, stubborn refusal to accept that this is how the story ends. The decision to rebuild.

---

### PART TWO — THE REBUILD
*Chapters 5–10*

**Chapter 5 — Starting From Voice**
When your hands are limited, your voice becomes your instrument again. Using voice commands, Talon, and a willingness to learn entirely new ways of working. The computer as an instrument. The first strange joy of making something new, in a new way.

**Chapter 6 — The First Architecture**
How NOIZY.ai was born. Not from a business plan but from a question: what do artists actually need? Sovereignty. Protection. A Voice Library that sounds like people, not machines. A DreamChamber that amplifies rather than replaces.

**Chapter 7 — The Three Critical Gaps**
The things nobody was building. The Ritual Prompts that capture the full humanity of a voice. The Autonomous Hunter that protects what artists create. The Living Data Protocol that lets artists stay in control of their own intelligence. Building the things that didn't exist yet.

**Chapter 8 — RSP_001**
Becoming your own proof of concept. The decision to make yourself the Model of the Year — not from ego, but from conviction. If it works for you, it works. And it worked.

**Chapter 9 — The Corpus**
What it looks like when one person — one voice, one set of ideas — generates a complete architecture for a creative economy. Forty, fifty, sixty documents. Each one a piece of the answer. Building the thing completely before asking anyone to believe in it.

**Chapter 10 — Canada**
The Fortress Economy. Why building in Canada matters. Why protecting Canadian creative culture is not small or parochial — it is a model for how every country in the world should protect what its artists make. The global story, seen from the North.

---

### PART THREE — THE GIFT
*Chapters 11–14*

**Chapter 11 — The Human Experiment**
Why all of this — every protocol, every platform, every document — is really about one thing: making sure people don't lose touch with what it feels like to be human. The five causes of disconnection. The six acts of restoration. The shovel, the mirror, the door.

**Chapter 12 — The Voice Library**
What it means to build something that sounds like a burned-out detective from Halifax, an old storyteller from Newfoundland, a fierce Gen Z from Lagos. Not perfect. Not neutral. Human. The boutique agency model as an act of cultural love.

**Chapter 13 — The Wisdom Project and the Kids**
Cross-generational connection as a design principle. Elder wisdom carried forward. Children given tools for expression before the system tells them what they should be. The long game.

**Chapter 14 — One Man's Love**
The only way to give something to the human race is to mean it. Not to pitch it. Not to monetize it first and hope the love follows. To mean it first, build it completely, and then let the work speak. What it means to be a Champion who does not argue — who just builds and hopes people recognize a great idea when they see one.

---

### EPILOGUE — What's So Funny?

Nothing.

There is absolutely nothing funny about peace, love and understanding. Nick Lowe knew it. Elvis Costello screamed it. Dave Edmunds played it straight. And after 15 months in the quicksand, in the tsunami, in the rebuild, in the long sleepless nights building documents and systems and tools and architectures — I can tell you with complete certainty:

We just need a little bit more of it.

That is why NOIZY.ai exists.
That is why any of this exists.
