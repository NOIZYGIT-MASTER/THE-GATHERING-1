I · THE WORLD THEY INHERITED

The Year Everything Was Being Taken

"To understand what Robert Stephen Plowman built, you must first understand what he was building against. The world of 2026 was one of the most extraordinary moments of creative abundance and creator poverty in all of human history — simultaneously."

In 2026, a musician could reach one billion ears. A voice actor could have their voice cloned by a platform they had never heard of, used in a film they would never be paid for, in a language they did not speak, heard by audiences who would never know their name. The technology had never been more powerful. The human behind it had never been more invisible.

The platforms of that era — many of whose names we still teach in the History of Extraction curriculum — had been designed with extraordinary precision to do one thing: convert human creative expression into platform revenue, at the lowest possible cost to the platform and with the highest possible ambiguity about what was owed to the creator.

Consent existed in documents that could be changed on a Tuesday. Royalties were fractions of fractions. Voices — the most intimate thing a human being possesses, the sound of their breath, their laughter, their grief — were being scraped, cloned, and deployed without a single conversation with the person who owned them.

We look back now, from a world where an artist's Voice Estate is a legal instrument recognized in 194 jurisdictions, and we sometimes struggle to feel the weight of what 2026 was. Try. Try to imagine that you could be replaced — your voice, your sound, the thing that makes you you in every conversation you have ever had — and the organization that replaced you would face no legal consequence, no ethical censure, and no financial obligation.

That was 2026. Into that world, Robert Stephen Plowman opened a window.
