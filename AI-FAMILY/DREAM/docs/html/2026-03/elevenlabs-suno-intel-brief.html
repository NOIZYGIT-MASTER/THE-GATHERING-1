  • DUBBING

  • SOUND EFFECTS
    Text-to-sound-effects generator. Describe a sound, get audio. Launched 2024. Positions them against audio production libraries.

// CONTROVERSIES + VULNERABILITIES — KNOW THESE
⚠ CONSENT CONTROVERSY
In 2023, ElevenLabs' voice cloning was used to create deepfake audio of Joe Biden, Taylor Swift, and multiple journalists — without consent. The platform introduced stricter verification, but the architecture remained ToS-based, not cryptographic. The consent problem is not solved — it is papered over. This is the most important thing to know when talking to anyone who uses or is considering ElevenLabs.
◎ CREATOR ROYALTY = $0
ElevenLabs pays 0% royalty to voice creators unless they are in the Voice Library marketplace — which pays a small revenue share that the platform controls unilaterally. ElevenLabs' business model requires creators to pay monthly fees to use their own cloned voice commercially. The creator is a customer, not a partner.
ℹ STRATEGIC POSITIONING
ElevenLabs is strong at enterprise synthesis, accessibility tools, and developer API. Their weakness is creator trust and long-term loyalty. Professional voice actors generally dislike them — they are seen as a threat, not a partner. This is NOIZY's opening. Every voice professional who is uncomfortable with ElevenLabs is a potential NOIZYVOX RSP.

// THE RIAA LAWSUIT — THIS IS YOUR MOST IMPORTANT TALKING POINT
🚨 $500M RIAA LAWSUIT — ACTIVE — FILED JUNE 2024
Sony Music, Universal Music Group, and Warner Records filed a $500M copyright infringement lawsuit against Suno and Udio (a competitor) in June 2024. The core allegation: Suno trained its model on copyrighted recordings without license, consent, or compensation to the musicians, songwriters, and labels who owned those recordings.

RIAA President Mitch Glazer called it "systematic theft on a massive scale." The complaint filed 1,700+ specific examples of Suno generating outputs that directly reproduced elements of copyrighted songs.

Suno's defense: claimed training on copyrighted data is "transformative fair use." Legal experts consider this defense weak. Outcome is pending but will be the most significant AI copyright decision of the decade.

The NOIZY angle: NOIZY's Never Clause NC_03 prohibits training on any voice without explicit opt-in consent. This lawsuit is the proof case for why that clause exists. NOIZY built the architecture this lawsuit was written about needing.
◎ NO HUMAN MUSICIAN IN THE LOOP
Suno's core vulnerability is that it produces music with zero human creative involvement and zero compensation to the musicians whose recorded work trained the model. It is the purest expression of the extractive AI model: ingest human creativity, generate synthetic output, monetize it, pay creators nothing.

In every conversation where someone mentions Suno, this is your differentiator: "Suno generates music without humans. NOIZY amplifies humans. Those are not the same category."
◎ UDIO — SUNO'S COMPANION LAWSUIT
Udio (another AI music generator, formerly Uncharted Labs) was sued in the same RIAA action. Udio raised $10M and generated similar controversy. Less market share than Suno but the same legal exposure. If you hear "Udio" — it's essentially the same story as Suno, same lawsuit, same vulnerability.

// SMART QUESTIONS TO ASK IN ANY MEETING — USE THESE
  • Q → "Have you looked at ElevenLabs?"
    "Yes — ElevenLabs is technically impressive and they've built real enterprise traction. Their challenge is the creator relationship. Their platform charges creators to use their own cloned voice commercially — 0% royalty flows back to the human. That's a fundamental tension that grows as creators get more sophisticated about their rights. NOIZY was built to solve that problem structurally, not just philosophically — the 75% royalty is hardcoded at the database layer. It can't be changed by a terms of service update."
  • Q → "How is this different from Suno?"
    "Different category entirely. Suno generates music with no human creative involvement — it's fully AI-generated. They're also currently facing a $500 million RIAA lawsuit from Sony, Universal, and Warner for training on copyrighted music without consent. NOIZY is the opposite: every sound starts with a human — a consented, watermarked, provenanced human voice and creative estate. NOIZY PROOF watermarking was built specifically so that what happened to the musicians in the Suno lawsuit can never happen to a NOIZY RSP."
  • Q → "ElevenLabs has a voice marketplace — isn't that what NOIZY does?"
    "ElevenLabs' Voice Library is a marketplace — you upload your voice, they take a platform cut, you earn a small share. The key differences: ElevenLabs controls the terms unilaterally, there's no cryptographic consent enforcement, and the voice estate ends when you stop paying your subscription. NOIZY's Voice Estate is permanent, inheritable, and the 75% split is in the database — not in the contract. Those are different levels of protection."
  • Q → "Could ElevenLabs or Suno just copy what you're doing?"
    "They could copy the features. They can't copy the architecture without restructuring their entire business model. ElevenLabs' revenue depends on creators paying subscription fees — if they moved to a creator-royalty model, their revenue curve inverts. Suno's model depends on training on existing music — if they added consent infrastructure retroactively, they'd need to re-license their entire training dataset. NOIZY is built consent-first from day one. Retrofitting consent onto an extractive model is like trying to add a foundation to a building that's already standing."
  • Q → "Are you worried about ElevenLabs' resources — they're a unicorn?"
    "Scale and trust are different things. ElevenLabs has $180M and 1 million users. They don't have the trust of the professional creator community. Voice actors, musicians, and podcasters who understand what's happening with their rights increasingly see ElevenLabs as a platform that profits from them rather than with them. NOIZY doesn't need to out-spend ElevenLabs. It needs to out-trust them. And that's already happening."
  • Q → "What do you think of Suno's accessibility argument — making music for everyone?"
    "It's a compelling framing with a real problem underneath it. The musicians whose work was used to train Suno's model didn't get to decide whether their art became a training dataset. That's the Suno paradox — they democratize music creation by first taking from the musicians who created it. NOIZY democratizes music creation by paying the humans who make it possible. The math is different and so is the relationship."
